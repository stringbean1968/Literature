\label{chap:Noise, Vibration and Hand-Arm Vibration Risk Assessment}

\section{Why this assessment matters in construction}

\paragraph{Noise-induced hearing loss (NIHL) and hand-arm vibration syndrome (HAVS) are two of the most prevalent, most preventable, and most consistently underassessed occupational diseases in the UK construction industry. Together with whole-body vibration (WBV) disorders, they represent a category of physical agent hazard whose consequences develop slowly and invisibly --- a worker does not notice the gradual deterioration of high-frequency hearing over ten years; an operative does not recognise the early tingling and blanching of vibration white finger as the precursor to irreversible nerve and vessel damage --- until the damage is permanent and the opportunity for prevention has long passed. The Control of Noise at Work Regulations 2005 and the Control of Vibration at Work Regulations 2005 established the legal framework for managing these physical agent hazards in UK workplaces, replacing earlier and less stringent provisions and setting clear exposure action and limit values that define the boundaries of acceptable worker exposure. Despite this framework, construction remains an industry in which workers routinely approach and exceed the exposure limit values for both noise and vibration, and in which the health surveillance necessary to detect early-stage HAVS and NIHL is chronically underimplemented.}

\paragraph{Noise on a construction site is not a single, characterisable hazard but a complex, variable, and directional sound field that changes with the activity, the site layout, the materials being worked, and the weather. A worker operating a breaker on a concrete floor for a full shift accumulates a daily noise dose that may reach or exceed the upper exposure action value of 85\,dB(A). A worker operating the same breaker for three hours and then spending the remainder of the shift 20 metres away from the nearest noisy plant accumulates a dose that may remain below the lower exposure action value of 80\,dB(A). The distinction matters: different regulatory duties apply at each action value, and the management approach must be proportionate to the actual exposure, not a generic ``high noise construction site'' determination. The HSE Noise Calculator tool, available online and free of charge, allows the competent assessor to combine the noise levels and durations of different noise sources encountered during a working day into a single daily noise exposure figure (LEP,d) expressed in dB(A), and to compare that figure against the regulatory action and limit values.}

\paragraph{Hand-arm vibration presents a particularly serious occupational health challenge in construction because the range of tools that generate significant vibration is very large --- angle grinders, disc cutters, breakers, needle scalers, chipping hammers, impact drivers, compaction plates, and floor saws all produce vibration magnitudes that can rapidly accumulate to the exposure action value of 2.5\,m/s\textsuperscript{2} A(8). The HSE HAVS Calculator allows the assessor to calculate the daily vibration exposure A(8) for combinations of tools used at different durations, and to identify the ``trigger time'' (the daily duration at which the exposure action value is reached for a specific tool) for each tool in the site's inventory. A worker using an angle grinder with a vibration magnitude of 8\,m/s\textsuperscript{2} reaches the exposure action value in approximately 28 minutes of trigger time; a worker using a breaker with a vibration magnitude of 20\,m/s\textsuperscript{2} reaches the exposure action value in approximately 7 minutes. These are not academic calculations --- they determine the health surveillance programme and the work rotation requirements that the risk assessment must specify.}

\paragraph{Whole-body vibration (WBV) affecting drivers and operators of construction plant is a less visible but equally real occupational health issue. Dumper operators traversing uneven haul routes, excavator operators working on rough ground, and plate compactor operators are all exposed to WBV that can cause or exacerbate lower back disorders over a working career. The Control of Vibration at Work Regulations 2005 set an exposure action value of 0.5\,m/s\textsuperscript{2} A(8) and an exposure limit value of 1.15\,m/s\textsuperscript{2} A(8) for WBV. HSE guidance document L141 and the HSE WBV Calculator provide the assessment framework for quantifying WBV exposure from construction plant, and the assessment must specifically address WBV for plant operators who spend the majority of their working day in the cab or seat of vibrating machines on rough ground.}

\barquote{``A construction worker who loses their hearing or develops HAVS during their working life does not lose it on the day of the incident --- they lose it over ten or twenty years of inadequately assessed and inadequately controlled exposure. The assessment that is not conducted today contributes to the disability that presents in a clinical consultation a decade from now.''}

\example{Breaker Operator Daily Noise Dose}{An operative is tasked with breaking up a 200\,mm reinforced concrete floor slab using a 30\,kg electric breaker. The breaker generates a noise level at the operator's ear of approximately 107\,dB(A) during operation. The HSE Noise Calculator indicates that operating this breaker for 11 minutes of effective trigger time would reach the upper exposure action value of 85\,dB(A) LEP,d. The operative is scheduled to break for 5 hours per 8-hour shift, pausing for approximately 15 minutes in every hour (standard practice for HAVS management). The effective trigger time is approximately 4 hours 15 minutes. The calculated LEP,d is in excess of 95\,dB(A) --- well above the exposure limit value of 87\,dB(A) and indicating a mandatory requirement for hearing protection and a management obligation to reduce exposure by substitution, engineering controls, or work rotation. The risk assessment specifies: Class 5 ear defenders with SNR of 34\,dB as mandatory PPE; acoustic enclosure around the breaking area; task rotation with a second operative taking alternate breaking shifts; acoustic silencer on the chipping plate where available; and a health surveillance audiometry programme for both operatives.}

\section{Legal and professional framework}

\paragraph{The Control of Noise at Work Regulations 2005 and the Control of Vibration at Work Regulations 2005 establish the regulatory framework for physical agent hazard management in UK workplaces, implementing the EU Physical Agents Directives. Both sets of Regulations set lower and upper exposure action values and exposure limit values, and prescribe the hierarchy of duties from risk assessment through technical and organisational controls to health surveillance. HSE guidance documents EH44, L140, L141, and EH57 translate the regulatory requirements into practical assessment and control guidance specific to construction operations.}

\paragraph{Key frameworks relevant to this chapter include: Control of Noise at Work Regulations 2005; Control of Vibration at Work Regulations 2005; Health and Safety at Work etc.\ Act 1974; Management of Health and Safety at Work Regulations 1999; EH44 (Controlling Noise at Work --- HSE guidance); L140 (Hand-Arm Vibration --- HSE guidance); L141 (Whole-Body Vibration --- HSE guidance); EH57 (Assessment of Exposure to Hand-Arm Vibration); HSE HAVS Calculator and Noise Calculator tools.}

\begin{itemize}
  \bitem{Control of Noise at Work Regulations 2005}{Establishes the lower exposure action value (LEP,d 80\,dB(A) or peak sound pressure 135\,dB(C)), the upper exposure action value (LEP,d 85\,dB(A) or peak sound pressure 137\,dB(C)), and the exposure limit value (LEP,d 87\,dB(A) or peak sound pressure 140\,dB(C)), accounting for the attenuation provided by hearing protection. Regulation 5 requires a suitable and sufficient risk assessment of noise exposure. Regulation 6 requires technical and organisational controls to reduce noise exposure to as low a level as is reasonably practicable. Regulation 7 requires hearing protection where the upper exposure action value is reached or exceeded. Regulation 9 requires health surveillance for workers whose noise exposure is at or above the upper exposure action value.}
  \bitem{Control of Vibration at Work Regulations 2005}{Establishes for HAV the exposure action value (A(8) of 2.5\,m/s\textsuperscript{2}) and the exposure limit value (A(8) of 5\,m/s\textsuperscript{2}), and for WBV the exposure action value (A(8) of 0.5\,m/s\textsuperscript{2}) and the exposure limit value (A(8) of 1.15\,m/s\textsuperscript{2}). Regulation 5 requires a suitable and sufficient assessment of vibration exposure. Regulation 6 requires technical and organisational controls. Regulation 7 requires health surveillance where vibration exposure is at or above the exposure action value. Regulation 8 imposes a specific duty to ensure that workers who may be at risk receive training and information on the nature of HAV and WBV hazards and the symptoms of HAVS.}
  \bitem{Health and Safety at Work etc.\ Act 1974}{Provides the overarching duty to protect the health of employees from work-related physical agent exposure. In the noise and vibration context, this duty requires the employer not only to implement the specific duties of the Noise and Vibration Regulations but also to ensure, through the management system, that the controls specified in the regulations are actually implemented and that health surveillance findings are acted upon.}
  \bitem{EH44 --- Controlling Noise at Work}{HSE guidance providing detailed technical guidance on measuring and assessing construction noise, selecting and fitting hearing protection, implementing engineering and organisational noise controls, and establishing hearing protection zones. EH44 is the primary reference for assessors conducting noise surveys on construction sites.}
  \bitem{L140 --- Hand-Arm Vibration}{HSE guidance covering the assessment of HAV exposure, the selection of low-vibration tools, the implementation of trigger-time controls, and the establishment of a HAVS health surveillance programme. L140 includes tool vibration magnitude data from the European VIBDATA database and the UK HSE tool database that can be used in place of site-measured vibration data for the initial risk assessment.}
  \bitem{L141 --- Whole-Body Vibration}{HSE guidance covering WBV exposure assessment for construction plant operators, including guidance on the selection of seats with low vibration transmissibility, the maintenance of haul routes to reduce WBV exposure, and the health surveillance requirements for workers at or above the WBV exposure action value.}
  \bitem{EH57 --- Assessment of Exposure to Hand-Arm Vibration}{HSE guidance providing the technical methodology for measuring HAV exposure in the workplace, including measurement of tool vibration magnitude, calculation of daily A(8) values, and interpretation of measured values against the regulatory action and limit values.}
  \bitem{HSE HAVS and Noise Calculator Tools}{Free online tools that allow risk assessors to calculate daily noise exposure (LEP,d) and daily vibration exposure A(8) from combinations of tool noise levels, vibration magnitudes, and daily trigger times, and to compare the calculated exposures against the regulatory values. These tools are the recommended starting point for initial risk assessment on construction sites where full noise and vibration surveys have not yet been conducted.}
\end{itemize}

\section{Conducting the assessment using the five-step method}

\subsection{Step 1 --- Identify Hazards}
\paragraph{Noise hazards in construction arise from a wide range of stationary and portable sources. Fixed plant noise sources include: concrete batching plants; tower crane electric motors; compressor sets; diesel generators; and concrete pump engines. Mobile plant noise sources include: tracked and wheeled excavators; dumpers and tipper trucks; road rollers; compaction plates; and concrete mixer trucks. Hand-held power tool noise sources include: angle grinders; disc cutters; cartridge-operated fixers; electric and pneumatic breakers; reciprocating saws; and nail guns. Process noise sources include: steel fixing and formwork striking; concrete placing and vibration; masonry cutting and sawing; and piling operations. HAV hazards arise from: breakers and chipping hammers; needle scalers and rotary scalers; angle grinders and disc cutters; compaction plates and rammers; road saws and floor saws; impact drivers and hammer drills; and screed vibrators. WBV hazards arise from: operators of dumpers, excavators, skid-steer loaders, road rollers, and forklift trucks driving on uneven haul routes or rough ground; and operators of pedestrian-operated compaction equipment who transmit whole-body vibration through extended handles.}

\subsection{Step 2 --- Decide Who or What Is Affected}
\paragraph{Persons at risk from noise hazards include: operatives directly operating noisy plant and tools; workers in proximity to noisy operations whose distance from the source provides insufficient attenuation to reduce their exposure below the exposure action values; site managers and supervisors who circulate in noisy areas as part of their monitoring role; delivery drivers attending the site during periods of intensive noisy operations; and adjacent property occupiers and members of the public near the site boundary. Persons at risk from HAV include all operatives regularly using vibrating hand tools, with particular attention to those who use multiple vibrating tools in succession during a single shift, since their cumulative exposure may exceed the exposure action value even if no single tool use episode would do so on its own. Persons at risk from WBV include all plant operators who spend significant time driving or operating vibrating machines on rough ground, and operatives using walk-behind compaction equipment.}

\subsection{Step 3 --- Evaluate Likelihood and Consequence}
\paragraph{NIHL and HAVS are irreversible conditions --- once the hearing is damaged or the vascular and neurological changes of HAVS are established, they cannot be cured. The consequence of inadequate noise and vibration controls over a working career is permanent disability, restriction of employment, and significant impact on quality of life including the ability to communicate in social settings, to hear warning signals and environmental sounds, and to use the hands in cold weather or under pressure conditions. The likelihood of NIHL arising from uncontrolled noise exposure on construction sites is high: many construction activities routinely generate noise levels between 100 and 115\,dB(A) at the operator's ear, and unprotected exposure at these levels exceeds the exposure limit value within seconds. The likelihood of HAVS arising from regular use of high-vibration-magnitude tools without trigger-time controls is equally high: tools with magnitudes exceeding 10\,m/s\textsuperscript{2} reach the exposure action value in trigger times as short as 15--20 minutes per day.}

\subsection{Step 4 --- Select and Implement Controls}
\paragraph{Controls for noise exposure must be selected by applying the hierarchy specified in the Control of Noise at Work Regulations 2005: technical measures first, then organisational measures, then hearing protection. Technical measures include: selecting quieter plant and tools at the procurement stage (the tool's vibration and noise data sheet, required by the Supply of Machinery Safety Regulations, should be reviewed before purchase or hire); fitting acoustic enclosures or silencers to stationary plant; using acoustic barriers and hoarding to reduce the propagation of construction noise to adjacent workers and to the community; maintaining plant to manufacturer's standards to prevent noise levels increasing due to wear. Organisational measures include: scheduling the noisiest activities for periods when the fewest workers are present; rotating operatives between noisy and quiet tasks to limit individual daily noise dose; locating static plant sources as far as possible from worker gathering areas. Hearing protection zones must be established around operations that consistently generate noise above 85\,dB(A) at working distances, and Class 3 or Class 5 hearing protection must be provided and its use enforced within those zones. For HAV: the primary control is reduction of trigger time using the HAVS Calculator to determine the maximum daily trigger time for each tool in the inventory at its measured or database vibration magnitude; task rotation must be implemented so that no operative approaches the exposure action value with a single tool; low-vibration alternative tools must be identified and specified wherever technically feasible.}

\subsection{Step 5 --- Record and Review}
\paragraph{Noise and vibration risk assessments must be reviewed when new tools or plant are introduced to the site, when the work activities generate higher or lower noise and vibration levels than assumed in the original assessment, when health surveillance findings indicate that any worker may be suffering NIHL or HAVS, and at the annual review cycle. The tool inventory noise and vibration data (from the tool data sheet or the HSE database) must be retained as part of the assessment record. Daily trigger-time records for HAV-at-risk workers must be maintained to allow the cumulative exposure to be monitored and to provide the health surveillance programme with the exposure history required for Tier 2 and Tier 3 HAVS health assessments. Any HAVS or NIHL finding on health surveillance must immediately trigger a review of the risk assessment for the affected worker's tasks and must result in a documented change to the controls before the worker is returned to the vibrating tool or noisy environment.}

\example{Five-Step Application}{A steelwork preparation contractor is using a pneumatic needle scaler to prepare steel beam surfaces for painting on a new bridge project. The needle scaler has a vibration magnitude of 16\,m/s\textsuperscript{2} and generates a noise level at the operator's ear of 105\,dB(A). Using the HSE HAVS Calculator, the exposure action value A(8) of 2.5\,m/s\textsuperscript{2} is reached in approximately 14 minutes of trigger time per day. Step 1 identifies: HAV from needle scaler; NIHL from needle scaler and co-located angle grinding. Step 2 identifies: scaler operator; adjacent angle grinder operative. Step 3 HAV exposure rated as Very High without controls; noise rated as Very High without controls. Step 4 implements: maximum daily trigger time of 14 minutes per operative established and recorded; task rotation between three operatives to distribute HAV exposure across the team, with no single operative exceeding 14 minutes effective trigger time; rotary disc scaler investigated as substitute (vibration magnitude 6\,m/s\textsuperscript{2}, extending trigger time limit to 60 minutes); hearing protection zone established with Class 5 ear defenders mandatory; noise survey confirms 105\,dB(A) within 3 metres --- 97\,dB(A) at 8 metres, confirming that the angle grinder operative requires hearing protection. Residual HAV risk assessed as Low following rotation; noise residual assessed as Low following hearing protection. Step 5: daily trigger-time log maintained; HAVS health surveillance Tier 1 pre-placement questionnaire completed; Tier 2 annual occupational health assessment scheduled.}

\section{Applying the hierarchy of controls}

\paragraph{The hierarchy of controls for noise and vibration places technical controls at the top of the practical control order. Hearing protection and anti-vibration gloves are PPE at the base of the hierarchy; they are mandatory when upper exposure action values are exceeded, but their adequacy depends on correct selection, correct fit, consistent use, and adequate attenuation, all of which can fail in the construction site environment. The risk assessment must demonstrate that the technical and organisational controls at the upper levels of the hierarchy have been genuinely evaluated and implemented before PPE is presented as the primary risk reduction measure.}

\begin{itemize}
  \bitem{Elimination}{Eliminate the noisy or vibratory process at the design stage: specify drill-and-split demolition methods in preference to pneumatic breakers for structural concrete removal; design structures with bolted connections rather than welded connections requiring weld-preparation grinding; specify non-percussive piling systems (bored, CFA, or screw pile) in preference to driven piling where ground conditions permit; design steelwork surfaces to be shop-primed, eliminating on-site surface preparation grinding.}
  \bitem{Substitution}{Substitute higher noise and vibration emission tools with lower emission alternatives: replace a petrol-driven angle grinder (vibration magnitude 8\,m/s\textsuperscript{2}) with a battery-operated equivalent (vibration magnitude 4--5\,m/s\textsuperscript{2}) for light-duty cutting work; replace a pneumatic breaker (noise 107\,dB(A)) with a hydraulic breaker fitted to the excavator arm (operator cab noise 78\,dB(A)) for bulk concrete demolition; replace a petrol plate compactor (vibration magnitude 12\,m/s\textsuperscript{2}) with a remote-controlled roller for area compaction tasks.}
  \bitem{Engineering controls}{Acoustic enclosures around static plant such as compressors, generators, and concrete batch plants; anti-vibration mounts on fixed plant bases; acoustic barriers and proprietary hoarding panels rated to BS EN ISO 11820 on site boundaries; cab suspension and anti-vibration seat suspension on mobile plant; tool-specific vibration data recorded and reviewed before tool hire or purchase; sound insulation on temporary site buildings that serve as welfare facilities near noisy operations.}
  \bitem{Administrative controls}{Hearing protection zones established and signed around all activities exceeding 85\,dB(A) LEP,d; daily trigger-time controls and records for all HAV-at-risk workers using the HAVS Calculator; task rotation schedules that distribute noise and vibration exposure across the workforce; maintenance schedules to prevent noise and vibration levels increasing above rated values due to wear, loose fixings, or damaged components; noise and HAVS awareness training for all operatives using significant noise or vibration sources; HAVS health surveillance programme with Tier 1, Tier 2, and Tier 3 assessments at the prescribed intervals.}
  \bitem{PPE}{Class 3 or Class 5 ear defenders within hearing protection zones (selected to provide the required attenuation without over-protection to the extent that operatives cannot communicate or hear warning signals); Class 1 SNR-rated earplugs as a supplementary option for extended duration, lower-noise work; anti-vibration gloves assessed against BS EN ISO 10819 for specific tools (noting that anti-vibration gloves are effective for some frequency ranges and not for others, and that their selection must be based on the vibration frequency spectrum of the specific tool); appropriate PPE to prevent cold and wet conditions that exacerbate HAVS symptoms, such as waterproof and insulating gloves for outdoor vibrating tool use.}
\end{itemize}

\section{Cadence of assessment and review triggers}

\paragraph{Noise and vibration risk assessments must be reviewed with a cadence that reflects the changing tool inventory, the changing work activities, and the dynamic outcomes of health surveillance. Unlike some hazard domains where a single comprehensive assessment covers a phase of work, the noise and vibration assessment must be responsive to the real-time addition of new tools and plant and to the health surveillance signals that may indicate the need for immediate control improvements.}

\begin{itemize}
  \bitem{When a new noisy or vibratory tool or machine is introduced to site}{Before any new tool or machine is introduced to the site, its noise and vibration emission data must be obtained from the manufacturer's data sheet or the HSE/VIBDATA database, and the risk assessment updated to include the new exposure contribution. This is a pre-introduction requirement, not a retrospective one.}
  \bitem{When health surveillance identifies an at-risk worker}{Any Tier 2 or Tier 3 HAVS health surveillance assessment that places a worker in HAVS Stockholm Workshop Scale Grade 2 or above, or any audiometry result that indicates significant deterioration in hearing threshold, must immediately trigger a review of the risk assessment for that worker's tasks. The worker must be removed from exposure above the exposure action value until the assessment review has been completed and enhanced controls have been implemented.}
  \bitem{At the start of each significant new work phase}{When the nature of the construction work changes from one phase to another --- from groundworks (heavy plant and compaction) to structural (concrete breaking and steel preparation) to M\&E (drilling, grinding, and pressing) to fit-out (nail guns, routers, and saws) --- the noise and vibration risk profile changes significantly. The assessment must be updated at each phase transition to reflect the new tool inventory and activity pattern.}
  \bitem{After maintenance or repair of plant and tools}{Plant and tools that have been repaired, reconditioned, or had components replaced may have different noise and vibration characteristics from those measured or recorded before maintenance. After any significant maintenance intervention, the tool's vibration magnitude should be confirmed against the manufacturer's specification and the assessment updated if the measured value has changed.}
  \bitem{At the annual review}{All noise and vibration risk assessments must be reviewed at a minimum annually even in the absence of a specific triggering event, to confirm that the tool inventory, worker exposure profiles, and control measures described in the assessment remain current and adequate.}
\end{itemize}

\section{Common failure modes}

\begin{itemize}
  \bitem{Vibration data not obtained for tools in use}{Risk assessments for HAV that state ``vibrating tools in use --- wear anti-vibration gloves'' without identifying the specific tools, obtaining their vibration magnitude data, and calculating the daily trigger-time limit for each tool are not suitable and sufficient assessments under the Control of Vibration at Work Regulations 2005. The vibration magnitude is the foundation of every HAV risk assessment; without it, no quantified exposure can be calculated and no health surveillance programme can be appropriately calibrated.}
  \bitem{Trigger-time limits specified but not monitored}{The specification of a trigger-time limit in a risk assessment --- ``no operative to use the breaker for more than 15 minutes per day'' --- provides no risk reduction unless the trigger time is actually monitored. Construction workers using vibrating tools do not carry stopwatches; without a supervisor actively monitoring and recording trigger times, operatives may use a high-vibration tool for 3 hours without anyone being aware that the exposure limit value has been exceeded multiple times.}
  \bitem{Health surveillance not implemented for at-risk workers}{HAVS health surveillance at Tier 1 (pre-placement questionnaire), Tier 2 (annual occupational health assessment), and Tier 3 (formal medical assessment by an occupational physician where Tier 2 indicates possible HAVS) is a regulatory requirement under the Control of Vibration at Work Regulations 2005 for workers whose HAV exposure is at or above the exposure action value. Failure to implement this programme means that early-stage HAVS --- which is reversible if exposure is stopped --- progresses to irreversible HAVS Grade 2 or 3 without detection. HSE prosecutions for HAVS failures consistently identify the absence of health surveillance as the critical management failure.}
  \bitem{Hearing protection not worn consistently despite provision}{The provision of ear defenders at the site welfare cabin is not equivalent to the wearing of ear defenders in the hearing protection zone throughout the noisy activity. Research consistently shows that hearing protection worn for only 95\% of the noise exposure duration provides less than half the calculated attenuation benefit. The management system must enforce the wearing of hearing protection throughout the hearing protection zone, not merely make it available.}
  \bitem{Anti-vibration gloves selected without reference to tool frequency spectrum}{Anti-vibration gloves assessed to BS EN ISO 10819 provide vibration attenuation across a frequency range of 31.5 to 1,250 Hz. However, the dominant frequency of vibration from different tool types varies considerably: percussive tools (breakers, chipping hammers) generate low-frequency vibration that anti-vibration gloves may not effectively attenuate; rotary tools (angle grinders, drills) generate higher-frequency vibration that the gloves may attenuate more effectively. Specifying ``anti-vibration gloves'' without reference to the tool's vibration frequency spectrum may result in gloves being worn that provide minimal or no meaningful HAV reduction.}
  \bitem{No noise survey conducted, noise levels estimated from generic tables}{Noise risk assessments based solely on generic published noise level tables without any site-specific measurement or tool-specific data may significantly underestimate or overestimate actual worker exposure. While generic data from the HSE Noise Calculator is an acceptable starting point, any assessment that identifies exposures near the action values must be confirmed by a noise survey conducted by a competent person using a calibrated noise dosimeter or sound level meter.}
\end{itemize}

\example{Failure Pattern}{A utilities contractor employs 12 operatives using pneumatic road breakers to open trenches for gas main replacement in an urban area over a 6-month programme. The risk assessment states: ``ear defenders must be worn; vibrating tools in use --- observe HSE guidelines.'' No tool vibration data has been obtained. No trigger-time limits have been specified or monitored. No health surveillance programme has been implemented. At the end of the 6-month programme, three operatives report tingling and numbness in their hands; one reports episodes of finger blanching when washing hands in cold water in the morning --- Stockholm Workshop Scale Grade 2 vasospastic HAVS. A subsequent HAVS health surveillance assessment by an occupational physician confirms HAVS in two operatives and borderline HAVS in a third. The contractor's risk assessment is reviewed; the pneumatic breakers used have vibration magnitudes ranging from 18 to 24\,m/s\textsuperscript{2}. The HAVS Calculator indicates that the exposure action value was reached in 8--15 minutes of daily trigger time with these tools, and that operatives regularly used the breakers for 4--5 hours per shift. The investigation identifies that the risk assessment failure --- the absence of tool-specific vibration data, trigger-time limits, and health surveillance --- allowed a preventable occupational disease to develop in three workers over six months of uncontrolled exposure.}

\begin{itemize}
  \bitem{Key takeaway 1}{NIHL and HAVS are irreversible occupational diseases; the assessment must be conducted before exposure occurs, not after symptoms are reported --- the window for prevention closes as the damage accumulates.}
  \bitem{Key takeaway 2}{Tool-specific vibration magnitude data from the manufacturer's data sheet or the HSE/VIBDATA database is the non-negotiable foundation of every HAV risk assessment; a generic assessment without tool-specific data cannot calculate trigger-time limits and cannot support a calibrated health surveillance programme.}
  \bitem{Key takeaway 3}{Trigger-time limits calculated from the HAVS Calculator must be actively monitored and recorded for every at-risk worker; a limit that exists only in the RAMS document but is not enforced in the field provides no protection against HAVS.}

  \bitem{Key takeaway 5}{Hearing protection must be worn throughout the full duration of exposure in the hearing protection zone; partial use of hearing protection provides a fraction of the calculated attenuation benefit and is inadequate to prevent NIHL at the noise levels generated by construction breaking, grinding, and cutting operations.}
