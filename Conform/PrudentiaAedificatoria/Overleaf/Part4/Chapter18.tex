\section{Why this assessment matters in construction}

Construction site fires are a major cause of financial loss and business interruption in the UK building industry, and a significant cause of personal injury and death among construction workers. Fire losses on construction sites regularly exceed hundreds of millions of pounds annually; the total economic impact, including programme delays, insurance costs, and the cost of reconstruction, dwarfs the direct fire damage. The causes of construction site fires --- hot work without fire watch, arson, electrical faults, and the accumulation of combustible materials in close proximity to ignition sources --- are all preventable through systematic fire risk assessment and management.

The construction site fire risk environment differs fundamentally from the fire risk environment in a completed building. Buildings under construction lack the passive fire protection (compartmentation, fire-rated linings, fire doors) that limits fire spread in completed buildings; they frequently contain large quantities of combustible materials (timber, insulation, packaging, bitumen felt, dry linings) that would not be present in normal occupation; they have temporary and incomplete means of escape that may change daily as construction progresses; and they may be occupied by workers who do not know the building layout well enough to find their way to exits in smoke conditions. The Regulatory Reform (Fire Safety) Order 2005 applies to non-domestic premises used as workplaces; the Construction Phase Plan required by CDM 2015 must address fire management arrangements.

Arson accounts for a significant proportion of construction site fires, particularly on urban sites, derelict building conversions, and sites that are accessible after working hours. Security measures that deter or prevent unauthorised access are therefore a component of fire risk management as well as being a site security measure. The interaction between fire risk and security risk requires that both are considered in the same assessment rather than as separate topics.

The fire risk assessment for a construction site is not a one-off exercise. The fire risk environment changes dramatically as construction progresses: the fire load increases as materials are delivered; the means of escape change as the building structure is erected; the fire risk from hot work fluctuates with the programme; and the risk from LPG changes as plant and temporary heating requirements evolve. The assessment must be dynamic, reviewed regularly, and updated to reflect material changes in the fire risk environment.

\barquote{``A construction site without a dynamic fire risk assessment is not a site where fire risk has been assessed --- it is a site where fire risk has been documented at one point in time and ignored thereafter.''}

\example{Site Reality}{A residential development is in its roofing phase. The main roof structure is complete in softwood timber; installation of the roof covering has begun but is not complete, leaving exposed timber for three weeks. Twelve houses share a common roofline. The contractor engages a disc-cutting operative to cut some metal roof fixings on a scaffold platform adjacent to the unprotected timber structure. Sparks from the cutting ignite dry shavings and debris that have accumulated in the roof void at the bottom chord level. The fire spreads in the roof void to four of the twelve houses before the fire service can contain it. The fire risk assessment for the roofing phase had not been updated to reflect the simultaneous presence of exposed timber and disc-cutting operations. The hot work permit system was not operational for disc cutting, only for torch work.}

\section{Legal and professional framework}

The Regulatory Reform (Fire Safety) Order 2005 (FSO) applies to all non-domestic premises used as workplaces, including the non-domestic parts of construction sites such as site offices, welfare cabins, and storage areas. The FSO requires that a responsible person carries out a suitable and sufficient fire risk assessment and implements appropriate fire safety measures. CDM 2015 requires the Construction Phase Plan to address fire management, including means of escape, fire detection and alarm, fire fighting equipment, and the hot work management arrangements.

HSE/BRE publication GE700 (Construction Site Fire Safety, produced by the Construction Industry Research and Information Association) provides the industry standard guidance for construction site fire risk management. Insurance requirements frequently specify additional standards that may exceed the regulatory minimum.

\paragraph{Key frameworks relevant to this chapter include: Regulatory Reform (Fire Safety) Order 2005; CDM 2015; MHSWR 1999; GE700 CIRIA Construction Site Fire Safety.}

\begin{itemize}
  \bitem{Regulatory Reform (Fire Safety) Order 2005}{Requires a suitable and sufficient fire risk assessment for all non-domestic premises, including construction sites; requires that fire safety measures are appropriate to the risk; requires that escape routes, emergency lighting, fire detection and alarm, and firefighting equipment are provided and maintained; requires that workers are trained in fire emergency procedures.}
  \bitem{CDM 2015}{Requires the Construction Phase Plan to address fire management, including: arrangements for discovering, reporting, and responding to fire; means of escape and evacuation arrangements; fire detection and alarm provision; firefighting equipment; hot work management; and arson prevention measures.}
  \bitem{MHSWR 1999}{Requires suitable and sufficient risk assessment of fire risks as part of the overall health and safety risk assessment for the construction site; the fire risk assessment forms a component of the overall site risk assessment and must be coordinated with it.}
  \bitem{CIRIA GE700}{Provides the construction industry standard guidance on fire risk assessment, fire prevention, fire detection, means of escape, firefighting, security, site organisation, and emergency planning for construction sites; compliance with GE700 principles represents the recognised industry best practice.}
\end{itemize}

\section{Conducting the assessment using the five-step method}

\subsection{Step 1 --- Identify hazards}
\paragraph{Fire hazards on construction sites include: ignition sources (hot work, electrical faults, arson, carelessly discarded smoking materials, friction from plant and machinery); fuel sources (combustible building materials including timber, insulation, plywood, OSB, cladding panels, and roofing felt; packaging materials; site waste; LPG and acetylene cylinders; diesel and petrol fuel; solvent-based products); and oxygen supply (ambient air in open structures; oxygen cylinders from welding operations). Specific fire risk scenarios in construction include: hot work sparks or radiant heat igniting adjacent combustible materials; subsurface smouldering in roof and floor build-ups following torch-on application; electrical fire from temporary supply faults; arson in unoccupied or overnight unsecured structures; heating appliance fire in temporary welfare facilities; and LPG or acetylene explosion from storage or handling failure.}

\subsection{Step 2 --- Decide who or what is affected}
\paragraph{Persons at risk from construction site fire include: all workers on the construction site and in welfare facilities; workers in adjacent trades who may be trapped by a fire originating in another part of the building; members of the public adjacent to the site perimeter; occupants of adjacent buildings affected by fire spread from the construction site; and emergency services personnel attending the fire. Special vulnerability applies to: workers in enclosed areas of the building with limited escape routes; workers using LPG-heated welfare facilities; workers performing lone or out-of-hours maintenance and security tasks.}

\subsection{Step 3 --- Evaluate likelihood and consequence}
\paragraph{The consequence of a construction site fire can range from contained local damage to total loss of the structure and injury or death of multiple workers. For an undetected fire in a combustible-loaded building under construction without permanent fire suppression, the consequence must be rated High to Very High (fatality, total loss). The likelihood of a fire in a typical construction project over its duration (given the prevalence of hot work, electrical installations, and combustible materials) is Medium to High without controls. Inherent risk is therefore High to Very High (16--25/25). A combination of hot work control, waste management, security, temporary fire detection, and maintained means of escape can reduce residual risk to Medium-Low.}

\subsection{Step 4 --- Select and implement controls}
\paragraph{Fire prevention controls: implement the hot work permit system with fire watch (Chapter~\ref{chap:Gas Safety and Hot Work Risk Assessment}); manage combustible materials on site --- specify weekly waste clearance and maintain a maximum fire load specification for each work area; prohibit smoking except in designated areas; implement site security measures to deter arson (security lighting, CCTV, hoarding, and out-of-hours security); store LPG, acetylene, and flammable liquids in designated external enclosures away from the main structure. Fire detection and response: install a temporary fire detection and alarm system appropriate to the stage of construction; identify and maintain clear means of escape at all stages of construction, including temporary escape stairs and routes; provide appropriate firefighting equipment (minimum one 9-litre water extinguisher per 200\,m$^2$ floor area; CO$_2$ for electrical areas); train all workers in evacuation procedures; conduct regular fire drills.}

\subsection{Step 5 --- Record and review}
\paragraph{The fire risk assessment must be reviewed and updated at each significant change in the construction programme: when the fire load changes materially, when the means of escape change, when a new phase of hot work commences, and after any fire or near-miss. The fire risk assessment must be available on site at all times. Fire drill records, extinguisher inspection records, and alarm testing records must be maintained.}

\example{Five-Step Application}{A steel-framed warehouse is under construction. The structure is complete; cladding installation is commencing. Extensive quantities of polyisocyanurate (PIR) insulation board are being installed within the metal cladding system. Step 1 identifies: PIR insulation is combustible; hot work (disc cutting of metal cladding) in close proximity to the PIR installation; temporary site office adjacent to the structure. Step 2 identifies: twelve cladding operatives; site manager in site office; delivery drivers. Step 3 rates inherent fire risk as Very High (25/25) given combustible insulation and hot work. Step 4 implements: hot work permit system operational for all disc cutting; PIR insulation boards stored covered and at maximum 200-board stack height; combustibles cleared from 5-metre zone around cutting operations; temporary fire detection system installed in the structure and site office; means of escape clearly marked and inspected weekly; fire extinguishers at all hot work locations and in site office. Residual risk rated Medium (12/25). Step 5: weekly fire risk walk with updated fire risk assessment.}

\section{Applying the hierarchy of controls}

\paragraph{The hierarchy for construction site fire risk management distinguishes between prevention (eliminating ignition and fuel combinations) and mitigation (limiting the consequences of a fire that does occur).}

\begin{itemize}
  \bitem{Elimination}{Reduce the fire load on the construction site: specify non-combustible construction materials where the design permits (concrete and steel rather than timber frame; non-combustible insulation rather than PIR); specify pre-finished and pre-glazed components to reduce on-site cutting operations.}
  \bitem{Substitution}{Specify cold-applied waterproofing systems rather than torch-on bitumen felt; specify press-fit pipe connections rather than soldered joints; reduce LPG use by specifying electric heating systems.}
  \bitem{Engineering controls}{Compartment construction sequences that limit the available fire load in any single unprotected area; temporary sprinkler systems in combustible-clad structures; temporary fire detection and alarm systems throughout the building; external secure LPG and flammable liquid storage cages.}
  \bitem{Administrative controls}{Hot work permit system with combustibles clearance and fire watch; weekly waste clearance programme; site security arrangements for arson prevention; fire emergency plan with trained assembly point wardens; regular fire drills; weekly fire risk assessment walk; extinguisher maintenance and testing; fire risk assessment review at each programme milestone.}
  \bitem{PPE}{Fire extinguisher competency training for all workers; fire marshal identification (high-visibility vest with fire marshal designation); communications equipment for contacting emergency services and coordinating evacuation.}
\end{itemize}

\section{Cadence of assessment and review triggers}

\paragraph{The dynamic nature of the construction site fire risk environment requires that the fire risk assessment is reviewed frequently; a static assessment prepared at the start of the project will be materially out of date within weeks.}

\begin{itemize}
  \bitem{At each programme milestone}{The fire risk assessment must be updated at each significant programme milestone: when the structure is enclosed, when the roof is completed, when services are installed, when finishing materials are introduced, and when the building is handed over for occupation.}
  \bitem{When the fire load changes materially}{When new combustible materials are delivered and stored within the structure, the fire risk assessment must be updated to reflect the increased fire load and revised fire prevention measures.}
  \bitem{Before and during hot work phases}{When a new phase of hot work commences (structural welding, torch-on roofing, brazing), the fire risk assessment must confirm that the hot work permit system is operational and that the clearance distances are appropriate.}
  \bitem{Following any fire, near-miss, or alarm activation}{Any fire event, near-miss, or false alarm activation must be investigated and the fire risk assessment reviewed before work resumes.}
  \bitem{At minimum monthly}{The fire risk assessment for ongoing projects must be formally reviewed monthly, with the weekly site safety inspection including a specific fire risk checklist covering means of escape, extinguisher condition, waste accumulation, and hot work permit compliance.}
\end{itemize}

\section{Common failure modes}

\begin{itemize}
  \bitem{Means of escape not maintained as construction progresses}{Escape routes that were adequate at the start of construction are blocked by material deliveries, partially-erected elements, or construction debris; no routine check of escape route condition is conducted.}
  \bitem{No temporary fire detection system installed}{The fire risk assessment identifies the need for a temporary fire detection system; it is not procured or installed; fire in the building overnight is not detected until the structure is well-alight.}
  \bitem{Hot work permit not applied to all ignition-source activities}{The hot work permit system is applied to LPG torch work and welding but not to disc cutting, angle grinding, or other spark-generating activities; fires ignited by disc cutting sparks occur outside the permit system.}
  \bitem{Combustible waste allowed to accumulate}{Construction waste, packaging, and off-cuts accumulate throughout the structure over weeks; the fire load available to a fire is dramatically increased; waste clearance is not carried out to the specified schedule.}
  \bitem{LPG stored inside the structure}{LPG cylinders are stored within the building for operational convenience rather than in external designated enclosures; a fire adjacent to or involving LPG cylinders produces a BLEVE (boiling liquid expanding vapour explosion) risk.}
  \bitem{Fire risk assessment not reviewed after significant programme changes}{A significant delay or acceleration in the programme changes the fire risk environment; the fire risk assessment is not updated to reflect the revised sequence and the associated change in fire load, escape routes, or hot work activity.}
\end{itemize}

\example{Failure Pattern}{A timber frame residential development is in its first-fix services phase. Fourteen houses are at the same stage: timber framed, roof covered in felt, external cladding not yet fixed, and first-fix electrical and plumbing work ongoing inside. An arsonist gains access to one of the houses through an unsecured window opening (glazing has not been installed). The fire spreads via the open roof space to three adjacent houses before it is contained; total loss of four houses results. The site fire risk assessment identified arson as a risk; the security measures specified (hoarding, padlocked site entrance) had not addressed the unglazed window openings in the timber-framed houses as arson access points.}

\begin{itemize}
  \bitem{Key takeaway 1}{The construction site fire risk assessment must be dynamic and updated at each programme milestone; a static assessment prepared at project start will not reflect the rapidly changing fire load, means of escape, and hot work activities of an active construction site.}
  \bitem{Key takeaway 2}{Hot work permit systems must cover all ignition-source activities including disc cutting and angle grinding, not only LPG torch work and welding; sparks from disc cutting are a leading cause of construction site fire.}
  \bitem{Key takeaway 3}{Arson prevention is a component of fire risk management; unglazed window openings, unsealed roof apertures, and accessible material stores are arson opportunities that must be addressed in the security assessment.}
  \bitem{Key takeaway 4}{Combustible waste accumulation dramatically increases the available fire load; weekly waste clearance must be enforced as a fire prevention measure, not simply as a housekeeping standard.}
\end{itemize}
