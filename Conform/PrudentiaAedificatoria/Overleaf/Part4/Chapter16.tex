\section{Why this assessment matters in construction}

Construction is one of the noisiest industries in the UK, and the occupational health consequences are permanent. Noise-induced hearing loss (NIHL) is the most prevalent occupational health condition in the construction sector after musculoskeletal disorders; it is irreversible, progressive, and entirely preventable. Once hair cells in the cochlea are destroyed by excessive sound energy, they do not regenerate. The tinnitus (permanent ringing in the ears) that accompanies occupational hearing loss compounds the disability and causes significant psychological distress, sleep disruption, and social isolation. The Control of Noise at Work Regulations 2005 establish the legal framework for noise management, but hearing damage in construction workers continues at a level that represents a systemic failure of the industry to apply the controls that are technically available and economically proportionate.

Hand-arm vibration syndrome (HAVS) is the second major occupational health condition in construction attributable to a specific physical agent. HAVS is caused by the transmission of vibration to the hands and arms through vibrating tools: angle grinders, breakers, disc cutters, floor grinders, compactors, chain saws, concrete pokers, road breakers, and similar equipment. HAVS causes irreversible damage to blood vessels, nerves, and the musculoskeletal tissues of the hands and fingers, producing vascular symptoms (vibration white finger, characterised by blanching of the fingers in the cold), neurological symptoms (tingling, numbness, and loss of manual dexterity), and musculoskeletal symptoms (hand and wrist pain, reduced grip strength). Like noise-induced hearing loss, HAVS is preventable through exposure control; like NIHL, once established, it cannot be reversed.

The Control of Vibration at Work Regulations 2005 establish the legal framework for vibration management, including the exposure action value (EAV) and the exposure limit value (ELV) for daily vibration exposure. The relationship between tool vibration magnitude (measured in m/s$^2$), daily usage duration, and the EAV and ELV is well established through the HSE's ready reckoner tools; a construction HAVS risk assessment must not guess at exposure levels but must calculate them using the declared vibration values for each tool type and the assessed daily usage durations.

Whole-body vibration (WBV) from the operation of plant --- excavators, dumpers, compactors, and rough-terrain vehicles --- is an additional vibration hazard that affects plant operators. WBV is associated with lower back pain, intervertebral disc damage, and associated musculoskeletal conditions. The Control of Vibration at Work Regulations 2005 apply equally to WBV.

\barquote{``HAVS and NIHL are both silent accumulations. The damage builds invisibly over years; by the time symptoms are reported, the injury is already permanent. Prevention and health surveillance are the only effective responses.''}

\example{Site Reality}{A groundworker has operated a road breaker for an average of three hours per day over a twelve-year career in highway maintenance. His employer has no HAVS assessment, no vibration exposure monitoring, and no health surveillance system. He begins to report occasional finger whitening in cold weather in year eight; he does not report it to his employer because he is unaware that it is work-related. By year twelve, he has stage 3 HAVS with bilateral blanching up to the second knuckle, persistent numbness in four fingers, and a grip strength reduction of 40 per cent. He is unable to continue as a groundworker. The investigation of his claim reveals that the road breaker he has used throughout his career had a declared vibration magnitude of 15\,m/s$^2$; at three hours' daily exposure, this equates to a daily exposure A(8) of 13.0\,m/s$^2$, compared with the ELV of 5\,m/s$^2$. He has been operating at more than twice the legal limit for over a decade.}

\section{Legal and professional framework}

The Control of Noise at Work Regulations 2005 apply to all employees exposed to noise at work and establish lower and upper exposure action values (LEAVs and UEAVs) and an exposure limit value (ELV). The LEAV is a daily or weekly noise exposure of 80\,dB(A) or peak sound pressure of 135\,dB(C); at this level, employers must make hearing protection available and provide information and training. The UEAV is 85\,dB(A) or 137\,dB(C); at this level, hearing protection must be worn, a hearing protection zone established, and health surveillance provided. The ELV is 87\,dB(A); exposure must not exceed this level even taking account of the protection provided by hearing protectors.

The Control of Vibration at Work Regulations 2005 establish the EAV for hand-arm vibration at 2.5\,m/s$^2$ A(8) (at which point health surveillance and control action must commence) and the ELV at 5\,m/s$^2$ A(8) (which must not be exceeded). For whole-body vibration, the EAV is 0.5\,m/s$^2$ A(8) and the ELV is 1.15\,m/s$^2$ A(8).

\paragraph{Key frameworks relevant to this chapter include: Control of Noise at Work Regulations 2005; Control of Vibration at Work Regulations 2005; MHSWR 1999; CDM 2015; HSE Noise and HAV ready reckoner tools.}

\begin{itemize}
  \bitem{Control of Noise at Work Regulations 2005}{Establish the LEAV (80\,dB(A)), UEAV (85\,dB(A)), and ELV (87\,dB(A)); require risk assessment of noise exposure; require elimination or reduction of noise exposure; require health surveillance above the UEAV; require hearing protection above the LEAV (mandatory above the UEAV); require designation of hearing protection zones.}
  \bitem{Control of Vibration at Work Regulations 2005}{Establish EAV (2.5\,m/s$^2$) and ELV (5\,m/s$^2$) for hand-arm vibration; require risk assessment using declared vibration values and HSE ready reckoner; require health surveillance above the EAV; require measures to eliminate or reduce vibration exposure; prohibit exposure above the ELV.}
  \bitem{HSE Ready Reckoner and Noise/HAV Exposure Tools}{Provide the standardised method for calculating noise and vibration exposure from declared tool emission values and daily usage patterns; use of these tools is the expected approach for construction HAVS and noise risk assessments.}
  \bitem{CDM 2015}{Requires designers to consider the noise implications of their specification choices (for example, specifying mechanical fixing over pneumatic fixing where appropriate) and to design out unnecessary noise and vibration generating activities where reasonably practicable.}
\end{itemize}

\section{Conducting the assessment using the five-step method}

\subsection{Step 1 --- Identify hazards}
\paragraph{Noise hazards on construction sites include: high-noise tools (angle grinders typically 100--105\,dB(A); disc cutters 105--110\,dB(A); road breakers 105--115\,dB(A); nail guns 150\,dB(C) peak; compressors 90--100\,dB(A) at operator position); plant operations (excavators 75--90\,dB(A) at operator position; compactors 85--100\,dB(A)); demolition activities including concrete breaking and structural demolition; cutting and grinding of masonry, metal, and timber; and sustained high-noise environments in which multiple sound sources combine. HAVS hazards include use of: road breakers, pneumatic chisels, and demolition hammers (typically 15--25\,m/s$^2$); angle grinders (5--10\,m/s$^2$); disc cutters (4--8\,m/s$^2$); concrete pokers (3--5\,m/s$^2$); petrol chain saws (5--10\,m/s$^2$); and plate compactors (2--7\,m/s$^2$).}

\subsection{Step 2 --- Decide who or what is affected}
\paragraph{Persons at risk from noise and vibration in construction include: operatives directly operating high-noise or high-vibration tools; workers in adjacent areas within the noise zone of high-noise activities; plant operators subject to cab noise and whole-body vibration; members of the public adjacent to construction sites for noise nuisance and potential noise-induced hearing loss from sustained long-term proximity (relevant for large-scale and long-duration projects adjacent to occupied premises). New workers and apprentices are at particular risk because their lifetime exposure accumulation begins from the first day of employment in a noisy trade.}

\subsection{Step 3 --- Evaluate likelihood and consequence}
\paragraph{Construction workers regularly using high-noise tools without hearing protection are very likely to exceed the UEAV of 85\,dB(A), and workers using road breakers and disc cutters without noise control are likely to exceed the UEAV within minutes of tool use. The consequence of sustained overexposure is permanent hearing loss and tinnitus. Similarly, workers using road breakers above the ELV for more than approximately 45 minutes per day will exceed the ELV without any additional controls. Inherent risk without controls is Very High (25/25) for high-noise and high-vibration tools. A combination of tool substitution, usage rotation, and hearing protection/anti-vibration gloves can reduce residual risk to Medium-Low (8/25); elimination of the highest-vibration tools from the task has the greatest potential for risk reduction.}

\subsection{Step 4 --- Select and implement controls}
\paragraph{Noise controls must follow the hierarchy: eliminate the noise source (avoid the task entirely); substitute with a less noisy method or tool (quieter abrasive discs, hydraulic rather than pneumatic breakers, diamond drilling rather than rotary impact); engineer (isolation of the noise source, enclosure of the tool, noise barriers, damping); administrative (limit time of exposure, rotate operatives, schedule noisy work during periods of minimum exposure to non-involved workers); PPE (hearing protection at the UEAV as a mandatory requirement with selection appropriate to the noise level). HAVS controls: substitute for lower vibration tools; use dampened anti-vibration tools; limit daily vibration exposure using the HAV ready reckoner to plan maximum daily durations; rotate operatives; maintain tools to manufacturer's specification (vibration increases with blunt cutting edges and worn anti-vibration mounts); health surveillance above the EAV.}

\subsection{Step 5 --- Record and review}
\paragraph{Noise and HAVS assessments must document the tool types, declared emission values, daily usage patterns, and calculated exposure levels. Health surveillance records must be retained. Tool maintenance records that affect vibration output must be available. The assessment must be reviewed when new tools are introduced, when health surveillance identifies an adverse finding, and at minimum annually.}

\example{Five-Step Application}{A groundworks contractor is breaking up a reinforced concrete slab using electrical demolition hammers for six hours per day over a two-week period. Step 1 identifies: demolition hammer noise at 108\,dB(A); hand-arm vibration magnitude of 12\,m/s$^2$. Step 2 identifies: two groundworkers using the hammers; site manager conducting inspections. Step 3 calculates: daily noise dose far above 87\,dB(A) ELV without hearing protection; daily HAV A(8) exceeds ELV 5\,m/s$^2$ after approximately 50 minutes at 12\,m/s$^2$. Step 4 implements: hydraulic breaker on excavator substituted for electric hammer breaking for the majority of the area; electric hammers used only for areas inaccessible to excavator; operatives limited to maximum 45 minutes per day on electric hammers; EN352 Class 4 earmuffs specified and hearing protection zone designated; anti-vibration tool handles fitted; health surveillance referral arranged for both operatives. Residual risk rated Low (6/25) after substitution. Step 5: daily HAV usage log maintained.}

\section{Applying the hierarchy of controls}

\paragraph{Noise and vibration controls share the same hierarchy; the greatest reductions are achieved through tool substitution and engineering controls rather than through PPE.}

\begin{itemize}
  \bitem{Elimination}{Avoid noise- and vibration-generating activities: specify factory-cut materials; use press-fit and mechanical connection systems rather than pneumatic fixings; design structures to avoid the need for large-scale concrete breaking.}
  \bitem{Substitution}{Replace high-vibration tools with lower-vibration alternatives: hydraulic rock breakers rather than hand-held road breakers; diamond core drilling rather than rotary impact; quieter abrasive discs with anti-vibration properties; mechanical compaction rather than plate compactors where the soil type permits.}
  \bitem{Engineering controls}{Noise enclosures around fixed cutting operations; acoustic barriers between noisy activities and adjacent occupied areas; anti-vibration tool mounts and dampened handles; regular maintenance of tool cutting edges and anti-vibration components.}
  \bitem{Administrative controls}{Planned tool rotation to limit individual daily exposure below the EAV/ELV; daily usage logs for high-vibration tools; health surveillance programme for all workers regularly exposed above the EAV; training in the risks of NIHL and HAVS, the importance of health surveillance, and the correct use of hearing protection; scheduling of high-noise activities away from sensitive receptors (residential properties, schools, hospitals) during working hours.}
  \bitem{PPE}{Hearing protection selected and worn at all noise levels above the LEAV; EN352 Class 4 or 5 earmuffs for the highest noise exposures; anti-vibration gloves (NB: anti-vibration gloves reduce vibration in the 150--1000\,Hz range but may be ineffective at lower frequencies; they are a supplementary, not primary, control for HAVS); hearing zone signage.}
\end{itemize}

\section{Cadence of assessment and review triggers}

\paragraph{Noise and vibration assessments must be reviewed as the tool fleet and activity programme changes; the health surveillance programme is the ongoing monitoring mechanism.}

\begin{itemize}
  \bitem{Before introduction of new tools or equipment}{Any new tool or equipment type must be assessed using its declared vibration/noise emission value before operatives are deployed; the HSE ready reckoner must be applied to calculate the maximum permitted daily usage duration.}
  \bitem{When health surveillance identifies an adverse finding}{Any health surveillance result indicating early HAVS or declining audiometric function must trigger an immediate review of the noise or vibration assessment and a reassignment of the affected operative pending clinical review.}
  \bitem{When activities change}{A change from low-vibration to high-vibration activities (e.g.\ commencement of road breaking or demolition) must trigger a specific risk assessment before the high-vibration activity commences.}
  \bitem{Following tool maintenance or repair}{Where tool maintenance has involved anti-vibration mount replacement or cutting element replacement, a post-maintenance check must confirm that vibration levels are within the assessed values.}
  \bitem{At minimum annually}{All noise and vibration assessments for ongoing projects must be reviewed annually to confirm currency.}
\end{itemize}

\section{Common failure modes}

\begin{itemize}
  \bitem{Exposure calculated by intuition rather than by ready reckoner}{HAVS assessments estimate exposure as ``moderate'' or ``high'' without applying the HSE ready reckoner to the declared vibration values and daily usage durations; the result is that operatives at or above the ELV are not identified.}
  \bitem{Health surveillance not arranged above the EAV}{The assessment acknowledges that operatives exceed the EAV but health surveillance is not arranged; the first indication of HAVS is the operative's own symptoms, by which time irreversible damage has occurred.}
  \bitem{Hearing protection available but not worn}{Earmuffs and earplugs are available at site induction but not enforced in hearing protection zones; operatives remove hearing protection because it makes communication difficult, and the enforcement of the requirement is inconsistent.}
  \bitem{Tool maintenance neglected}{Blunt cutting edges, worn anti-vibration mounts, and unbalanced cutting discs increase both vibration emission and noise output; regular maintenance is not conducted and tool vibration levels exceed the declared emission values used in the assessment.}
  \bitem{Whole-body vibration of plant operators not assessed}{HAVS assessment covers hand-held tools but does not address the WBV exposure of excavator, dumper, and compactor operators; the Control of Vibration at Work Regulations 2005 apply to both.}
  \bitem{Daily HAV usage not monitored}{The assessment specifies maximum daily usage durations for high-vibration tools; no mechanism exists to monitor whether operatives are exceeding their assessed daily limit.}
\end{itemize}

\example{Failure Pattern}{A utility contractor carries out water main relaying over a rolling three-year contract. Road breaker usage is the primary activity for breaking the carriageway. The HAVS risk assessment uses a vibration magnitude of 8\,m/s$^2$ based on a tool brochure value for a medium-sized breaker; the tool actually in use is a larger model with a declared value of 18\,m/s$^2$ and worn anti-vibration mounts. No daily usage logs are maintained. After two years, seven of twelve regular road breaker operatives report HAVS symptoms; health surveillance confirms stage 2 or 3 HAVS in four. The contract is investigated; the combination of incorrect vibration value, unreplaced anti-vibration mounts, and absence of usage logging has resulted in exposures three to four times above the ELV sustained over two years.}

\begin{itemize}
  \bitem{Key takeaway 1}{NIHL and HAVS are both irreversible; the purpose of the risk assessment is to prevent exposure, not to document it after the damage is done; health surveillance is the early warning system.}
  \bitem{Key takeaway 2}{Vibration exposure must be calculated using the HSE ready reckoner with the declared emission values for each specific tool model; a generic ``medium vibration'' classification is not an acceptable risk assessment approach.}
  \bitem{Key takeaway 3}{Health surveillance for all workers regularly exceeding the EAV for noise or vibration is a legal requirement; the health surveillance findings are the feedback mechanism that validates or invalidates the controls.}
  \bitem{Key takeaway 4}{Tool substitution (hydraulic breaker instead of hand-held road breaker; diamond drilling instead of impact drilling) provides the greatest reduction in vibration exposure and must be considered before PPE is specified.}
\end{itemize}
