\section{Why this assessment matters in construction}

Confined space incidents produce a pattern of multiple fatalities that is unique among construction hazards. It is common for the first casualty in a confined space incident to be an operative who enters a space with an oxygen-deficient or toxic atmosphere; it is equally common for subsequent fatalities to be persons who entered the space to attempt a rescue without atmospheric monitoring equipment, without breathing apparatus, or without adequate rescue equipment. The Confined Spaces Regulations 1997 recognise this rescuer-fatality pattern explicitly and require that rescue arrangements are in place before any confined space entry is made.

The range of confined spaces encountered in construction is broad and frequently unrecognised by the operatives who enter them. An inspection chamber or manhole is obviously a confined space; less obviously, so is a newly constructed basement with inadequate ventilation, a partially-completed water tank, a section of pipe or duct being worked on from both ends, a trench or excavation in which oxygen depletion or toxic gas accumulation is possible, or any enclosed space in which flammable vapours from stored chemicals or bitumen processing could accumulate to a dangerous concentration. The failure to recognise a space as a confined space before entry is as dangerous as the failure to control a recognised confined space, because the operative who does not know they are entering a confined space will not have atmospheric monitoring equipment, emergency rescue equipment, or a standby person.

Oxygen deficiency is the most common atmospheric hazard in construction confined spaces. Oxygen concentrations below 19.5 per cent cause increasing cognitive impairment; below 16 per cent, muscle function deteriorates; below 10 per cent, death occurs within minutes. Oxygen depletion occurs through: biological oxidation in soils and within sewers (carbon dioxide replaces oxygen); rusting of steel surfaces in enclosed metal tanks or pipes; reaction of dry ice (solid carbon dioxide used for chilling) with air in enclosed spaces; and displacement by inert gases (nitrogen, argon) used for welding or purging operations. In none of these scenarios does the atmosphere look different from normal air; the only reliable indicator is a direct-reading atmospheric monitor.

Toxic gas accumulation --- most commonly hydrogen sulphide (H$_2$S) from sewage and drainage systems, and carbon monoxide from engine exhausts in enclosed spaces --- represents a second category of confined space atmospheric hazard. H$_2$S in concentrations above approximately 100\,ppm causes rapid incapacitation; at 1,000\,ppm, it is immediately fatal. Carbon monoxide from a petrol generator or diesel compressor operating in or adjacent to an enclosed space can accumulate rapidly to life-threatening concentrations without any sensory warning (CO is colourless, odourless, and tasteless).

\barquote{``The most dangerous moment in a confined space rescue attempt is the decision to enter without monitoring equipment, driven by the imperative to help a colleague who cannot be seen to breathe. Three rescuers have died making that decision before the emergency services arrive.''}

\example{Site Reality}{Two drainage operatives are cleaning a 600-diameter foul sewer running beneath a residential road. The sewer is approximately 2.5 metres below ground and has been isolated from flow. One operative descends into the inspection chamber to begin mechanical cleaning; the other attends at the surface. Three minutes after descending, the operative at depth stops responding to communication. The surface operative, seeing no movement, descends to assist. Both operatives are found unconscious by the emergency services. The atmosphere in the chamber contains 9\% oxygen and 3,500\,ppm H$_2$S. Both operatives are resuscitated; one suffers permanent neurological damage. The investigation finds that no atmospheric monitoring was conducted before entry, no breathing apparatus was available, no rescue equipment was deployed, and no standby person with emergency rescue capability was in attendance. The permit-to-enter had been completed but the atmospheric monitoring equipment had not been brought to site that day.}

\section{Legal and professional framework}

The Confined Spaces Regulations 1997 are the primary legislative instrument for confined space management in construction. They apply to all confined spaces in which a specified risk exists, including oxygen deficiency, flammable atmosphere, toxic gas or vapour, free-flowing solid that may engulf, liquid that may engulf, excessive heat, or any other risk that could cause loss of consciousness or drowning. The Regulations impose a hierarchy identical to the general hierarchy: avoid confined space work where reasonably practicable; where it cannot be avoided, carry out a safe system of work including atmospheric monitoring, rescue arrangements, and a permit-to-enter; and ensure adequate rescue arrangements are in place before entry commences.

PUWER 1998 applies to all confined space equipment including atmospheric monitors, breathing apparatus, rescue tripods, and winches. CDM 2015 requires the Construction Phase Plan to address the management of confined space entry activities. The Health and Safety at Work etc.\ Act 1974 imposes the overarching duty of care.

\paragraph{Key frameworks relevant to this chapter include: Confined Spaces Regulations 1997; PUWER 1998; MHSWR 1999; CDM 2015; HSE L101 Safe Work in Confined Spaces.}

\begin{itemize}
  \bitem{Confined Spaces Regulations 1997}{Require avoidance of confined space work where reasonably practicable; require that where confined space entry cannot be avoided, a safe system of work (including atmospheric monitoring, emergency rescue arrangements, and a standby person) is in place; require adequate rescue arrangements before entry; require competency of all persons involved in confined space work including the supervisor, the entrant, and the standby person.}
  \bitem{HSE L101 Safe Work in Confined Spaces}{Provides the definitive guidance for confined space management; defines what constitutes a confined space; provides guidance on atmospheric monitoring, emergency rescue, permit systems, competency, and training requirements; specifies the responsibilities of the supervisor, entrant, and standby person.}
  \bitem{PUWER 1998}{Applies to all confined space equipment; atmospheric monitors must be in calibration and tested before each use; breathing apparatus must be maintained, inspected, and tested; rescue tripods, winches, and harnesses must be inspected before each use; all equipment must be suitable for the atmospheric conditions anticipated.}
  \bitem{CDM 2015}{Requires the Construction Phase Plan to address the management of confined space work on the project; the Pre-Construction Information must identify known confined spaces in the existing structure; the designer must consider whether the design can eliminate confined space requirements.}
\end{itemize}

\section{Conducting the assessment using the five-step method}

\subsection{Step 1 --- Identify hazards}
\paragraph{Confined space hazards in construction include: oxygen deficiency from biological oxidation, rusting, or gas displacement in enclosed spaces; H$_2$S and other toxic gases from sewage systems, drainage connections, and chemical decomposition in soil; carbon monoxide from combustion engines operating in proximity to the confined space; flammable atmosphere from solvent vapour accumulation (paints, adhesives, bitumen), methane from drainage connections, or hydrocarbon contamination in soils; drowning risk from unexpected water ingress or flooding of the space; engulfment from collapse of the space surrounding the entrant; excessive heat in spaces adjacent to heat sources or in elevated external temperature; and inability to self-rescue due to the geometry of the space (restricted entry or egress points).}

\subsection{Step 2 --- Decide who or what is affected}
\paragraph{Persons at risk from confined space hazards include: the operative or operatives entering the space; the standby person at the entry point (who may be exposed to escaping gas); rescue team members who may enter the space in an emergency; emergency services personnel attending a confined space incident; and persons in the vicinity if an explosive atmosphere within the space ignites. Members of the public may be at risk if a sewer or drainage system connects to accessible manholes in the area.}

\subsection{Step 3 --- Evaluate likelihood and consequence}
\paragraph{Entry into an oxygen-deficient or toxic atmosphere confined space results in rapid incapacitation and death; the consequence is Very High (5/5). The likelihood of oxygen depletion or toxic gas accumulation in drainage systems, newly-constructed enclosed spaces, and spaces in contaminated ground is Medium to High without atmospheric monitoring, placing inherent risk at Very High (20--25/25). With atmospheric monitoring before entry, continuous monitoring during work, trained confined space operatives, and emergency rescue arrangements in place, residual risk can be reduced to Low (4--6/25).}

\subsection{Step 4 --- Select and implement controls}
\paragraph{The primary control for confined space risk is to avoid the confined space entry entirely: can the task be accomplished from outside the space using remote tools, cameras, or extended-reach equipment? Where entry cannot be avoided, the safe system of work must include: atmospheric testing before entry (4-gas monitor: O$_2$, LEL, CO, H$_2$S as minimum); continuous atmospheric monitoring by the entrant during work; a trained and equipped standby person at the entry point throughout the entry; a documented rescue procedure with identified rescue team, rescue equipment (tripod, winch, harness, breathing apparatus), and emergency services contact; a permit-to-enter issued by a competent person before each entry; training and competency verification for all entrants, standby persons, and supervisors.}

\subsection{Step 5 --- Record and review}
\paragraph{Confined space permit-to-enter records must be retained for the project duration. Atmospheric monitor calibration records must be maintained. Rescue equipment inspection records must be retained. Any near-miss or incident involving a confined space must be recorded, investigated, and reported under RIDDOR where applicable. The risk assessment must be reviewed before each entry in spaces where conditions may have changed since the previous entry.}

\example{Five-Step Application}{A mechanical contractor is constructing a below-ground pump chamber in a commercial development. The completed chamber is 3.0 metres deep, 2.5 metres by 3.0 metres in plan, with a single 600-mm access opening. Step 1 identifies: oxygen depletion from fresh concrete curing within the sealed chamber; potential for CO accumulation if diesel plant operates near the opening; H$_2$S from a drainage connection stub within the chamber. Step 2 identifies: two mechanical operatives entering to install pump station equipment; supervisor above. Step 3 rates inherent risk as Very High (25/25) without monitoring. Step 4 implements: 4-gas atmospheric monitor tested and calibrated before each entry; readings confirmed before entry (O$_2$ above 19.5\%; LEL below 5\%; CO and H$_2$S below respective WELs); trained standby person at the access opening throughout each entry with communication maintained; rescue tripod, winch, and escape breathing apparatus stored at the access point; permit-to-enter issued and signed; all plant removed from proximity of access opening during entry. Residual risk rated Low (4/25). Step 5: atmospheric monitor readings logged for each entry.}

\section{Applying the hierarchy of controls}

\paragraph{The Confined Spaces Regulations 1997 embed the hierarchy of controls as the primary regulatory framework: avoid the entry; if it cannot be avoided, implement the full safe system of work; ensure rescue arrangements are in place before any entry commences.}

\begin{itemize}
  \bitem{Elimination}{Avoid confined space entry entirely: use remote cameras and inspection equipment; use long-reach tools for cleaning, inspection, and maintenance; design the permanent works to provide adequate access without confined space classification (e.g.\ enlarged access opening, external access platform).}
  \bitem{Substitution}{Replace work tasks requiring entry with equivalent tasks achievable from outside: CCTV survey rather than visual inspection; robotic cutting or cleaning rather than manual entry; remote-operated equipment for valve adjustment or instrument reading.}
  \bitem{Engineering controls}{Forced ventilation of the space before and during entry to maintain oxygen levels; inert gas purging and flushing before entry where toxic contaminants are present; physical isolation of all services (gas, water, electricity) to the space before entry.}
  \bitem{Administrative controls}{Permit-to-enter system with atmospheric monitoring requirement; training and competency verification for all entrants and standby persons; rescue procedure documented and briefed before each entry; emergency services contact arrangements confirmed; communication maintained throughout entry; prohibition of solo confined space entry.}
  \bitem{PPE}{Calibrated 4-gas personal monitor worn by the entrant throughout the work; escape breathing apparatus (ELSA, 10-minute escape set) for entrants where atmospheric risk cannot be fully confirmed by monitoring alone; full-body harness and lifeline for non-horizontal confined space entry; confined space rescue tripod, winch, and stretcher at the access point.}
\end{itemize}

\section{Cadence of assessment and review triggers}

\paragraph{Confined space assessments must be reviewed before each entry because atmospheric conditions change; the permit-to-enter is the operational mechanism of the risk assessment at entry level.}

\begin{itemize}
  \bitem{Before each entry}{Atmospheric monitoring must be conducted before each entry into any confined space, regardless of the findings of the previous entry; conditions change between entries and a space that was safe on one occasion may be immediately dangerous on the next.}
  \bitem{When the space or its connections change}{Any change in the connections to the space (new drainage connection, new gas or fluid pipe), any physical modification of the space, or any change in adjacent activities that may affect the space atmosphere must trigger an atmospheric reassessment before entry.}
  \bitem{After any abnormal atmospheric reading}{If atmospheric monitoring detects an oxygen concentration below 19.5\%, an LEL above 5\%, or any CO or H$_2$S above the short-term WEL, entry must not proceed until the cause is identified and remedied; the risk assessment must be reviewed before the next entry.}
  \bitem{Following any confined space incident or near-miss}{Any incident in which a person has been exposed to a hazardous confined space atmosphere, or any near-miss, must trigger an immediate review of the risk assessment, the permit system, and the rescue arrangements.}
  \bitem{At the start of each new work phase in the space}{When a new activity commences within the confined space (new materials used, new tools introduced), the atmospheric risk must be reassessed to confirm that the new activity does not introduce a new hazard (e.g.\ introduction of solvent-based products into a previously inert space).}
\end{itemize}

\section{Common failure modes}

\begin{itemize}
  \bitem{Atmospheric monitoring not conducted}{Atmospheric monitoring equipment is not brought to site, or is brought but not tested, or readings are taken outside the space rather than at the base of the entry point where conditions are most hazardous. This is the single most dangerous failure mode.}
  \bitem{Monitor not calibrated or out of calibration}{Atmospheric monitors are not calibrated at the intervals specified by the manufacturer or are used beyond their calibration service date. An out-of-calibration monitor may not respond accurately to the atmospheric conditions it is measuring.}
  \bitem{No standby person}{Confined space entry proceeds without a trained standby person at the access point. When the entrant becomes incapacitated, no one is present to initiate rescue; the first indication of a problem is when the entrant fails to return at the end of the task.}
  \bitem{No rescue equipment at the entry point}{Rescue equipment (tripod, winch, harness) is not available at the entry point; when rescue is required, operatives attempt an unequipped rescue by entering the space without breathing apparatus.}
  \bitem{Permit-to-enter not issued for familiar spaces}{Operatives who work regularly in a specific confined space develop familiarity with the space and stop issuing permits, conducting atmospheric monitoring, or deploying rescue equipment. Familiarity breeds complacency; conditions in the space change unpredictably.}
  \bitem{Space not recognised as a confined space}{The space meets the definition of a confined space under the Confined Spaces Regulations 1997 but is not recognised as such; operatives enter without any confined space controls because they believe the definition does not apply.}
\end{itemize}

\example{Failure Pattern}{A drainage contractor is installing a sump pump in a below-ground plant room within an industrial building. The plant room is accessed via a vertical ladder and a 600-mm access hatch; it measures approximately 4.0 metres by 3.0 metres and contains two existing drainage sumps. The contractor does not classify the plant room as a confined space because ``it's a room, not a confined space''. Two operatives enter the plant room; the sumps have accumulated a layer of sludge that is releasing H$_2$S. One operative is overcome within three minutes. The second exits to raise the alarm. Emergency services attend and recover the incapacitated operative using breathing apparatus; he is unconscious for 20 minutes and suffers a hypoxic brain injury. The plant room meets the definition of a confined space in every respect.}

\begin{itemize}
  \bitem{Key takeaway 1}{Atmospheric monitoring before every confined space entry is a non-negotiable requirement; conditions in the same space can change dramatically between entries; there are no ``safe'' spaces that can be exempt from monitoring.}
  \bitem{Key takeaway 2}{A trained and equipped standby person at the access point is required for every confined space entry; attempted unequipped rescue by colleagues is the most common cause of multiple fatalities in confined space incidents.}
  \bitem{Key takeaway 3}{Rescue equipment (tripod, winch, harness, escape breathing apparatus) must be positioned at the access point before entry commences, not retrieved from stores when an emergency is identified.}
  \bitem{Key takeaway 4}{Familiarity with a confined space is a risk factor, not a protective factor; operatives who enter familiar spaces without monitoring or controls because they ``know it is safe'' are responsible for a significant proportion of confined space fatalities.}
\end{itemize}
